Creation of the ARPANET

By Riley Palomata

The steps to start the ARPANET may have been started with Paul Baran and his ideas of packet switching but the person to imagine the ARPANET was J. C. R. Lickider in a research paper which described a "Galatic Network" of interconnected computers1. Lickider worked for the government agency ARPA (hence the name ARPANET) and evetually became the first director of ARPA's Information Processing Techniques Office (IPTO). Another individual who joined the ARPA team to work on the ARPANET was Robert Taylor. He joined ARPA in 1965 and in 1966 he became the director of IPTO. During his time working at ARPA he saw thateach mainframe or computer that IPTO was funding "had its own sense of community and was digitallyisolated from the other one2 ." As director he saw that "if ARPA's scattered computers could be linkedtogether, hardware, software, and data could be efficiently pooled among contractors rather than wastefully duplicated2 ."

The director who actually oversaw the completion of the ARPANET was Lawrence Roberts a researcher from MIT who was strong armed with the threat of funding cuts from ARPA to join the project3.. Roberts was the one to decide that the network would be a distributed packet switching system like the one Paul Baran imagined for his communication network.
